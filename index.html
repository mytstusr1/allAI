| 🎓 Teacher's Guide

🎓 Teacher's Guide

You Don't Need to Be a Tech Expert 👋

You just need to stay a few steps ahead, and there are amazing resources built exactly for this. Here's your complete roadmap.

Step 1 — Learn the Basics Yourself First

Start here — these require zero math or coding background:

Resource What it is Link Cost
Google's "How AI Works" Visual, beginner-friendly intro ai.google/education ↗ ✓ Free
Elements of AI Free online course, no math needed elementsofai.com ↗ ✓ Free
AI4K12 Initiative Made specifically for K-12 teachers ai4k12.org ↗ ✓ Free
"AI for Everyone" by Andrew Ng Plain-English overview, 6 hours coursera.org ↗ ✓ Free to audit
🎯 Goal:

Understand the big ideas — what is AI, how does machine learning work, what are its limits and ethical concerns.

Step 2 — Learn the 5 Big Ideas of AI (Your Curriculum Framework)

The AI4K12 initiative (the gold standard for K-12 AI education) organizes everything around 5 Big Ideas:

🤖

Perception

How computers sense the world (images, speech, text)

🔁

Representation & Reasoning

How AI stores and uses knowledge

📚

Learning

How machines learn from data (the heart of ML!)

🤝

Natural Interaction

How humans and AI communicate

⚖️

Societal Impact

Ethics, bias, and real-world consequences

💡 Note: These 5 ideas work for any age group — just adjust the depth.

Step 3 — Pick the Right Tools for Your Students' Age

Ages 5–8: Unplugged / No Screens Needed

  • "AI Unplugged" activities — teach AI concepts through games and storytelling, no tech required
  • Teachable Machine ↗ — drag photos, train a model in minutes! ✓ Free
  • Sorting games to explain how AI categorizes things

Ages 9–12: Visual & Block-based

  • Scratch + ML extension — build simple AI projects visually ✓ Free
  • Machine Learning for Kids ↗ — incredibly teacher-friendly ✓ Free
  • LearningML.es ↗ — visual ML platform with no coding ✓ Free

Ages 13+: Intermediate — Full Plans in the Tabs Above!

  • Teachable Machine ↗ — train image/sound/pose models in a browser ✓ Free
  • MIT App Inventor + AI ↗ — build real apps with AI features ✓ Free
  • Python with pandas/sklearn for older, more advanced students ✓ Free

Step 5 — Don't Forget Ethics — It's the Most Important Part

🎯 Teaching kids HOW AI works is only half the job. Teach them to ask:
🤔

Who made this AI, and why?

📊

What data was it trained on? Could it be biased?

👥

Who could be helped or hurt by this AI?

🔒

What happens to our privacy?

Resources:
  • Moral Machine MIT ↗ ✓ Free
  • Day of AI ethics modules ✓ Free
  • AI4K12 societal impact lessons ✓ Free

Step 6 — Your Week-by-Week Starting Plan

Weeks 1–2

Take "Elements of AI" course yourself

Week 3

Explore AI4K12 and pick your grade band

Week 4

Play with Teachable Machine & ML for Kids

Week 5

🎉 Run your first lesson with students!

Step 7 — Key Mindset Shifts

✅ You don't need to know HOW to code — focus on concepts and critical thinking
✅ AI education is as much about ethics and society as it is about tech
✅ Kids often learn faster than you — let them explore and teach you back!
✅ Mistakes and confusion are part of the learning — model curiosity, not expertise
♿ Show how AI helps people — text-to-speech, captioning, vision tools are great examples
🌍 AI needs people from EVERY background — artists, historians, lawyers, engineers. There's a role for everyone.

import tensorflow as tf from tensorflow import keras # Load MNIST handwritten digits dataset (X_train, y_train), (X_test, y_test) = keras.datasets.mnist.load_data() X_train, X_test = X_train / 255.0, X_test / 255.0 # Normalise # Build the neural network model = keras.Sequential([ keras.layers.Flatten(input_shape=(28, 28)), keras.layers.Dense(128, activation="relu"), keras.layers.Dropout(0.2), # Prevent overfitting keras.layers.Dense(64, activation="relu"), keras.layers.Dense(10, activation="softmax") # 10 digit classes ]) model.compile(optimizer="adam", loss="sparse_categorical_crossentropy", metrics=["accuracy"]) history = model.fit(X_train, y_train, epochs=10, validation_split=0.1) print(f"Test accuracy: {model.evaluate(X_test, y_test)[1]:.2%}")
